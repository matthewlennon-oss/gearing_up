Wheel & Axle Investigation Lab - Teacher Guide

This guide is based on the ideal-machine model used in the simulation. It does not include friction, bending or slipping.

Cover Lesson Flow

TimeStudent actionCover teacher prompt
0-5 minOpen the simulation and worksheet.Check every pair has Force Lab visible.
5-10 minComplete Q1-Q4.Ask students to point to the wheel, axle, effort and load.
10-25 minRun Force Lab trials."What stayed the same, and what changed?"
25-37 minRun Speed Lab trials."Did the rotation count change?"
37-50 minComplete application, challenge and exit response."Which number is your evidence?"

Expected Answers

Q1: axle; together; force.

Q2: B.

Q3: Award one mark each for wheel, axle, effort force and load/resistance force.

Q4: Required effort force decreases. The effort point travels farther.

Q6: As wheel radius increased from 4 cm to 12 cm, the required effort decreased from 30 N to 10 N.

Q7: The larger wheel gives a smaller effort force, but the effort point travels farther. It trades force for distance.

Q9: A.

Q10: False. The attached wheel and axle complete the same number of rotations. The larger rim travels farther in the same time.

Q11: Larger steering wheel. It reduces the effort needed, although hands move through a greater distance.

Q12: Force multiplier: screwdriver handle, doorknob, steering wheel. Speed multiplier: motor turning a ceiling-fan axle.

Q13: 10 cm. At 10 cm the required effort is 12 N, which meets the limit. At 8 cm the effort is 15 N, which is too high. A 12 cm wheel also works, but it is not the smallest available successful choice.

Q14: Award marks for a sensible task, valid radii, a correct benefit and a correct trade-off.

Misconceptions to Probe

"The large wheel turns more times." Ask the student to compare the two rotation counters.

"The load became lighter." Ask which reading shows the unchanged 60 N load.

"The biggest wheel is always best." Ask whether hand travel, size and effort location matter for the chosen task.
